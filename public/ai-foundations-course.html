1 How it actually works

It predicts the next word — it doesn’t “know” things

Underneath, the AI is a very sophisticated autocomplete. It looks at everything so far and guesses what word is most likely to come next, over and over. Understanding this one thing explains almost everything else.

Watch it think — tap a word to continue the sentence
Your question: “Tell me about a small animal that sat down.”

Notice the percentages shift with the question. Your prompt is the context the model weighs up — it tilts the odds toward words that fit what you asked, which is why a clear question pulls the answer in the right direction.

Because it’s predicting plausible text rather than retrieving facts, it can sound completely confident and still be wrong. Fluent ≠ correct.
2 The unit it reads in

It doesn’t see words — it sees tokens

Before the AI can predict anything, your text is chopped into small pieces called tokens. A token is roughly a word, but long or unusual words split into several. The model reads and writes one token at a time — and you’re often billed per token, so they matter.

Tap a phrase to see how it breaks into tokens
Pick a phrase above.

Common words are usually one token. Rare or long words (“unbelievable”, “tokenisation”) get split into chunks. Spaces and punctuation count too.

Tokens are why models have a length limit (a “context window”) and why cost scales with how much you put in and get out. Shorter, clearer text = fewer tokens.
3 Confident, but sometimes wrong

It can invent facts that look completely real

When the AI doesn’t know something, it doesn’t stop — it generates the most plausible-sounding answer. These invented details are called hallucinations: fake quotes, fake citations, fake numbers, all delivered with total confidence.

The fix isn’t to distrust everything — it’s a habit: for anything that matters (names, numbers, dates, citations), assume it could be invented until you’ve checked.

6 Don’t expect one perfect shot

It’s a conversation, not a search box

The best results come from back-and-forth. Treat the first answer as a starting point, then steer: shorter, warmer, “you missed this”. Crucially, the AI remembers everything earlier in the conversation — so each reply builds on the last instead of starting fresh.

Steer the draft — tap a follow-up
You
Write a thank-you note to my team.
AI
Dear Team, thank you for all your hard work this quarter. Your dedication is greatly appreciated. Best regards.
Why this works: because the AI holds the whole thread in memory, you never re-explain. “Make it shorter” only makes sense if it remembers what to shorten — that retained context is what turns a back-and-forth into a genuinely better answer.
The first draft is raw material. Refining through dialogue is the skill — and the AI’s memory of the conversation is what makes each step count.
7 Not all AI is equal

8 The human stays the owner

It’s an assistant — you’re always the owner

You always have the final say. The question isn’t who’s allowed to decide — that’s always you — it’s where the AI is the better-suited first hand and where the work is best led by you. Set each task to whichever is the more effective fit.

Whichever way you set these, you remain the owner of every output. “AI-suited” means it’s an efficient first pass — never that you’ve handed over responsibility.
Lean on the AI where it’s the efficient first hand. Keep the judgment, the checking, and ownership with you. That’s the whole game.
